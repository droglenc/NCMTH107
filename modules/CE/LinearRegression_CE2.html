Deer Fawns

Wildlife ecologists in Texas wanted to determine if the number of fawns born to each doe could be explained by the doe’s body mass (Ginnett and Young 2000). As part of their study, the researchers recorded the mean number of fawns born to a doe (over a period of time) and the body mass of the doe (kg). Use the results in the following graph to explain the relationship to answer the questions below.

  1. Which is the explanatory variable?
  2. Which is the response variable?
  3. Express the equation of the best-fit line in terms of the variables of the problem.
  4. Interpret the slope of the best-fit line in terms of the variables of the problem.
  5. If a doe weighed 45 kg, how many fawns on average would you expect her to have?
  6. If a doe weighing 32 kg gave birth to an average of 1.9 fawns, what is the residual for this doe?
  7. What is the correlation coefficient between mean number of fawns born and doe body mass?
  8. How much of the variability in the mean number of fawns born is explained by knowing the body mass of does?
  9. If body mass increases by 5 kg, how many more fawns can you expect that doe have?
  10. Do you have any concerns about the strength of this relationship?
