Identifying Type I and II Errors

Note:
  • In these two questions, I would first write the H0 and HA as in the last module. I would then write these hypothesis in words relative to the research hypothesis. For example, in the first question, H0 is that the chicken is not contaminated whereas HA is that the chicken is contaminated. I then write the definitions of Type I (H0 is true, but it is rejected) and Type II (H0 is false, but is not rejected) errors, but with the wordy meanings of the hypotheses substituted in appropriately.
  1. The H0: μ=15.4 and HA: μ>15.4. A Type I error is saying that the chicken is contaminated (rejecting H0) when it really is not contaminated (H0 is true). A Type II error is saying that the chicken is not contaminated (not rejecting H0) when it really is contaminated (H0 is false). In my mind, a Type II error is more egregious as people would be eating contaminated chicken that they have been told is not contaminated.
  2. The H0 is that the drug does not exceed the specification whereas the HA is that the drug does exceed the specification. A Type I error occurs if the drug is not bad (H0 is true) but it is concluded that is bad (rejecting H0). A Type II error occurs if the drug is bad (H0 is false) but it is concluded that the drug is not bad (not rejecting H0). In my mind, a Type II error is more egregious as people would be using a “bad” drug that they were told was not bad.

How to Reduce β

Note:
  • In question 1 below you are asked to prove that the rejection wall is at 97 (within rounding). This requires finding the value of the sample mean that has α “more extreme” (as dictated by the HA) on the “null distribution” (sampling distribution assuming that HO is true). This is always a reverse calculation and, given this HA, is a reverse-left calculation.
  • In question 2 below you are asked to prove that β is 0.137 (within rounding). This requires finding the area in the “Do Not Reject H0” region of “actual distribution” (sampling distribution assuming that μ equals the actual mean provided). This is always a forward calculation and always in the opposite direction of the reverse calculation in quation 1; thus, a forward-right calculation in this instance.
