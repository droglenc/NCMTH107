Note:
  • Make sure to describe your evidence in Step 3 for why you are performing a chi-square test. It is not adequate to simply say “because there is a categorical response variable and two or more groups are being tests.” At least say what the response variable is and what the groups are.
  • When testing the assumptions (Step 5), make sure to explicitly say that there are more than five in each cell of the expected table. It is not adequate to simply make the expected table.
  • In hand-calculation questions (e.g., hurricanes) make sure to put lower.tail=FALSE in distrib() when calculating the p-value (Step 8) in a chi-square problem. This is true for EVERY chi-square problem.
  • You values may differ slightly from mine as I calculate everything in R that holds many decimal places on intermediate values.

Hurricane Strengths

  1. α=0.10.
  2. HO: “The distribution of hurricantes into the strength categories is the same for both time periods” versus HA: “The distribution of hurricantes into the strength categories is NOT the same for both time periods”
  3. A chi-square test is required because a categorical response variable with three levels (strength category) from two groups or populations (time periods) was recorded.
  4. This is an observational study without obvious randomization.
  5. The test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a chi-square distribution because all cells in the expected table (Table 1) have values greater than five.

    6. Table 1: Expected frequency table for distribution of hurricanes into the strength and time period categories.

              Cat 1&2 Cat 3 Cat 4&5
1901-1950    51.4  25.4     8.1
1951-2000    43.6  21.6     6.9
  6. The table of observed frequencies were given as in Table 2.

    7. Table 2: Observed frequency table for distribution of hurricanes into the strength and time period categories.

              Cat 1&2 Cat 3 Cat 4&5
1901-1950      51    26       8
1951-2000      44    21       7
  7. The χ2 test statistic is 0.0382 with 2 df (Table 15).
  8. The p-value is 0.9811.
  9. The HO is rejected because the p-value<α.
  10. It appears that the distribution of hurricanes into the strength categories is the same for both time periods (Table 3).

    11. Table 3: Row percentage table for the observations of hurricane strengths by time period.

              Cat 1&2 Cat 3 Cat 4&5 Sum
1901-1950    60.0  30.6     9.4 100
1951-2000    61.1  29.2     9.7 100

R Appendix.

library(NCStats)
distrib(0.0382,distrib="chisq",df=2,lower.tail=FALSE)

Popularity in Middle School

  1. α=0.05.
  2. HA: “The distribution of students into the goals categories differs among grades” and HO: “The distribution of students into the goals categories DOES NOT differ among grades”.
  3. Chi-square test because (i) the response variable (goals category) is categorial and (ii) two or more groups or populations (grades) were sampled.
  4. This is an observational study because the students were not allocated to grades. The students were not obviously randomly selected (and were likely part of a voluntary response survey).
  5. All cells in the expected frequency table (Table 4) were greater than 5; the assumptions for a chi-square test have been met.

    6. Table 4: Expected frequency of students (assuming that Ho is true) by stated goal for three grades.

         goals
grade   Grades  Popular   Sports
    4 61.49163 35.10251 22.40586
    5 90.94561 51.91632 33.13808
    6 94.56276 53.98117 34.45607
  6. The observed frequency table is shown in Table 5.

    7. Table 5: Observed frequency of students by stated goal for three grades.

         goals
grade Grades Popular Sports
    4     63      31     25
    5     88      55     33
    6     96      55     32
  7. χ2=1.3121 with 4 df (Table 6).

    8. Table 6: Chi-square test results for testing that the distribution of students by goals differs among grades.

    Pearson's Chi-squared test with obs 
X-squared = 1.3121, df = 4, p-value = 0.8593
  8. p-value=0.8593 (Table 6).
  9. Do not reject H0 because the p-value>α.
  10. It appears that the distributions of goals by students does not differ by grade. Analysis of the row percentage table (Table 7) suggests that grades are the primary goal for each grade.

    11. Table 7: Row percentage table for goals by grade of the student.

R Appendix.

d <- read.csv("https://github.com/droglenc/NCData/raw/master/PopularKids.csv")
obs <- xtabs(~grade+goals,data=d)
chi1 <- chisq.test(obs,correct=FALSE)
chi1$expected
chi1
percTable(obs,margin=1,digits=1)

  1. α=0.01.
  2. HO: “The distribution of people in the race categories is the same for males and females” versus HA: “The distribution of people in the race categories is NOT the same for males and females.”
  3. A chi-square test is required because a categorical response variable with four levels (race/ethnicity) from two groups or populations (sex) was recorded.
  4. This is an observational study without obvious randomization.
  5. The test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a chi-square distribution because all cells in the expected table (Table 8) have values greater than five.

Forest Composition

  1. α=0.05.
  2. HA: “The distribution of stands into the times since last fire differs among stand types” and HO: “The distribution of stands into the times since last fire DOES NOT differ among stand types”.
  3. Chi-square test because (i) the response variable (time since last fire) is categorial (in the way it was recorded) and (ii) two or more groups or populations (stand types) were sampled.
  4. This is an observational study because the stand type was not set or manipulated by the researcher. The stands were not obviously randomly selected.
  5. All cells in the expected frequency table (Table 11) were NOT greater than 5. The p-value calculation below is suspect.
