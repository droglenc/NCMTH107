Popcorn Yield by Variety

  1. α = 0.10.
  2. The H0:μw-μy=0 where μ is the mean number of unpopped kernels, w represents white kernels, and y represents yellow kernels (thus, positive numbers represent more unpopped white kernels). The HA:μw-μy≠0.
  3. A two-sample t-test is required because (i) a quantitative variable (kernels unpopped) was measured, (ii) two groups/populations (yellow and white) exist, and (iii) those populations were independent (there is no connection between all of the individuals of white corn and those of yellow corn).
  4. The data appear to be part of an observational study with the individuals randomly selected.
  5. Individuals in the groups/populations are independent (as stated above). The sample size (nw+ny=60) is ≥ 40. The population variances appear to be equal because the p-value for Levene’s test (given as 0.972) is “large” (i.e., > α). Therefore, the test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a t-distribution with nw+ny-2 = 58 df.
  6. The statistic is x̄w-x̄y = 4.267-3.567 = 0.700. The pooled sample variance is \(s_{p}^{2}=\frac{(30-1)4.456^{2}+(30-1)4.485^{2}}{30+30-2}\) = 19.986. The standard error of the statistic is \(SE_{\bar{x}_{w}-\bar{x}_{y}}=\sqrt{19.986\left(\frac{1}{30}+\frac{1}{30} \right)}\) = 1.154.
  7. The test statistic is t = \(\frac{0.700-0}{1.154}\) = 0.606 with 58 df.
  8. The p-value is 0.5469.
  9. The H0 is not rejected because p-value > α.
  10. There does not appear to be a difference in the mean number of unpopped kernels between the white and yellow varieties.
  11. One is 90% confident that the difference in mean number of unpopped kernels between the white and yellow varieties is between -1.610 and 3.010 (as computed with 0.700±2.002*1.154). This further illustrates that there is not a difference in mean number of unpopped kernels between the white and yellow varieties (because 0 is in the confidence region).

Heating System Efficiency

  1. α = 0.10.
  2. The H0:μe-μt=0 where μ is the mean BTUs and the subscripts represent the two types of vents (positive numbers represent more BTUs used for houses fit with electric vent). The HA:μe-μt≠0.
  3. A two-sample t-test is required because (i) a quantitative variable (BTUs) was measured, (ii) two groups/populations (electric and thermal vents) exist, and (iii) those populations were independent (there is no connection between all of the vents in the two groups).
  4. The data are part of an observational study where it is not obvious that the devices were randomly allocated to houses.
  5. The individuals in the two groups are independent (no vent was placed in the same house). The sample size (ne+nt = 90) is ≥ 40. The population variances appear to be equal because the p-value for Levene’s test of the homogeneity of variance test (given as 0.996) is “large” (i.e., > α). Therefore, the test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a t-distribution with ne+nt-2 = 88 df.
  6. The statistic is x̄e-x̄t = 9.908 - 10.143 = -0.235. The pooled sample variance is \(s_{p}^{2}=\frac{(40-1)3.020^{2}+(50-1)2.767^{2}}{40+50-2}\) = 8.305. The standard error of the statistic is \(SE_{\bar{x}_{e}-\bar{x}_{t}}=\sqrt{8.305\left(\frac{1}{40}+\frac{1}{50} \right)}\) = 0.611.
  7. The test statistic is t = \(\frac{-0.235-0}{0.611}\) = -0.385 with 88 df.
  8. The p-value is 0.7012.
  9. The H0 is not rejected because the p-value > α.
  10. There does not appear to be a difference in average BTUs between houses equipped with the electrically and the thermally activated vent.
  11. One is 90% confident that the difference in average BTUs between houses equipped with the electrically and the thermally activated vent is between -0.875 and 0.405 (as computed with -0.235±1.662*0.385). This further indicates that there is no difference in average BTUs between houses equipped with the electrically and the thermally activated vent (as 0 is in the confidence region).

Dioxins in Milk Cartons

  1. α = 0.01.
  2. The H0:μL-μUL=0 where μ is the mean dioxin level, L represents lined, and UL represents unlined cartons (thus, negative numbers represent lower dioxin levels in the lined cartons). The HA:μL-μUL<0 (representing that the lined cartons will reduce the dioxin level).
  3. A two-sample t-test is required because (i) a quantitative variable (dioxin level) was measured, (ii) two groups/populations (cartons with and without foil) exist, and (iii) those populations were independent (there is no connection between all of the cartons in the two groups).
  4. The data appear to be part of an observational study; however there is no mention of randomization in this study although it would have been easy to randomly select cartons.
  5. The individuals in the two groups are independent (cartons could not be both linead and unlined). The sample size (nL+nUL=100) is ≥ 40. The variances appear to be UNequal because the Levene’s test p-value (<0.00005) is less than α. Even though the assumptions are not fully met in this case, I am going to continue with the analysis and assume that the test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a t-distribution with nL+nUL-2 = 98 df.
  6. The statistic is x̄L-x̄UL = 0.006 - 0.029 = -0.023 (Table 1).
  7. The test statistic is t = -17.541 with 98 df (Table 1).
  8. The p-value is <0.00005 (Table 1).
  9. The H0 is rejected because the p-value < α.
  10. The lined milk cartons have lower levels of dioxin, on average, than the unlined cartons.
  11. One is 99% confident that the mean dioxin levels is more than 0.02 GREATER in the unlined than in the lined cartons (Table 1).

Pork Quality

  1. α = 0.05.
  2. The H0:μ1-μ2=0 where μ is the mean backfat thickness and the subscripts represent the two diets (thus, positive numbers represent more backfat thickness for pigs fed diet 1). The HA:μ1-μ2≠0.
  3. A two-sample t-test is required because (i) a quantitative variable (backfat thickness) was measured, (ii) two groups/populations (different diets) exist, and (iii) those populations were independent (there is no connection between all pigs in the two diet groups).
  4. The data are part of an experimental study where the pigs were randomly allocated to treatments.
  5. The individuals in the two groups are independent (no pig was fed both diets). The sample size (n1+n2=25) is < 40 but ≥ 15 and the sample distributions (i.e., histograms) are not strongly skewed. The population variances appear to be equal because the p-value for Levene’s test of the homogeneity of variance test (given as 0.532) is “large” (i.e., > α). Therefore, the test statistic computed below should reasonably follow a t-distribution with n1+n2-2 = 22 df.
  6. The statistic is x̄1-x̄2 = 3.420-2.989 = 0.431. The pooled sample variance is \(s_{p}^{2}=\frac{(12-1)0.295^{2}+(12-1)0.375^{2}}{12+12-2}\) = 0.1138. The standard error of the statistic is \(SE_{\bar{x}_{1}-\bar{x}_{2}}=\sqrt{0.1138\left(\frac{1}{12}+\frac{1}{12} \right)}\) = 0.1377.
  7. The test statistic is t = \(\frac{0.431-0}{0.1377}\) = 3.13 with 22 df.
  8. The p-value is 0.0049.
  9. The H0 is rejected because the p-value < α.
  10. There does appear to be a difference in average backfat thickness between pigs fed the two diets, with pigs fed diet 2 having a lower mean backfat thickness and, thus, a higher-quality pork.
  11. One is 95% confident that the mean backfat thickness on all pigs fed diet 1 is between 0.145 and 0.717 cm greater than the mean backfat thickness of all pigs fed diet 2 (as computed with 0.431±2.074*0.138). Thus, diet 2 produces higher-quality pork.
